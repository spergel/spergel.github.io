Futurism: A Legacy of Failure

Futurism's promise of radical change collapsed under the weight of its own contradictions, leaving a legacy of complicity, not revolution. Their vaunted rebellion dissolved into shameful subservience, a cautionary tale of how easily the avant-garde can become a sad echo of the establishment it once scorned.

To embrace 'Futurism' now is to inherit this legacy of artistic compromise and ideological bankruptcy. It's to willingly don the mantle of a movement whose future already spectacularly failed, dissolving into irrelevance and moral compromise.

The 21st century demands more than ghosts from a failed revolution.

Still Calling Yourself a Futurist?

Futurism's original scream? A calculated bid for relevance, more shock tactic than genuine revolution. Invoking it today is anachronistic masturbation over a failed revolutionary movement. Sounds a bit like being a Socialist, huh?

You might be clinging to the label if:

  • Your 'innovation' is mistaking noise for novelty, just as they did.
  • Your 'critique' is a fresh coat of paint on their attention-grabbing aesthetics.
  • Your 'artistic energy' is spent agitating within acceptable bounds for the establishment's approval.
What Was Futurism?

Futurism began in 1909 with a scream—Marinetti's "Manifesto of Futurism." Their entire ideology was based on explosive and unconstrained artistic energy. It was anti-clerical, anti-democracy, and proud.

As soon as they got close to power, they bit their tongue on all their principles and became the establishment's strongest defenders. Even before the Fascist takeover in 1922, the Futurists surrendered their authenticity to Mussolini, even helping write the Fascist manifesto of 1919.

What did they get in return?

Stripped of genuine vigor and influence, their grand project devolved into manufacturing trivial culture wars. Their final, hollow gesture was a crusade against pasta to promote Mussolini's autarkic agricultural policy.

Their zeal extinguished, their compromises mounted:

  • Sacred Art: From vowing to burn churches to begging to bathe them in neon for Mussolini's approval.
  • Academic Revolt: From demanding the demolition of academies to accepting sinecures within them.
  • Anti-Capitalist Stance: From communalist nationalism to begging Mussolini to let shopowners have neon signs in piazzas, and begging clergy to reconsider a ban.
“Pastasciutta, therefore, is an absurd Italian gastronomic religion.” — F.T. Marinetti on his "war on pasta"
